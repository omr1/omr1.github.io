    Dental Radiologist: Salary, Duties and Requirements
    Considering a career as an OMR?

    Information for individuals interested in pursuing oral and maxillofacial radiologist:

    Salary: 168,870
U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) doesn’t provide salary information for dental radiologists specifically
The average salary for dental specialty for dentists in 2013 was $168,870 in 2013

    Typical work day

    •	Dependent on patient
•	Usually uses x-rays, CT scans, MRI, PET scans of the head and neck region
•	Work with other dentists to conduct clinical exams
•	Most OMRs are situated in dental schools
•	Dental education including digital image production, subtraction radiography, CT, MRI and other techniques
•	Recently, emphasis on problem based and case based learning in many schools
	enables OMR to integrate instruction in image production with interpretation of 
	disease, thereby combining radiology with oral pathology, diagnosis and medicine
•	OMR faculty member might teach a class, supervise students in radiographic technique
•	In clinic, review radiographic interpretation of images with students as they evaluate their patients
•	Perform a consultation on a challenging case
•	Research and provision of specialized radiographic services for referral patients

    Types of patients

    •	Provides radiographic services beyond the scope of general dentists and other specialist
•	Referral of patients for tomographic evaluation of the TMJ or prospective processing 
	workstation are found at many facilities and allow school based OMR to deliver 
	more sophisticated services in TMJ or implant imaging
•	Clinical exams to find and evaluate diseases, tumors or conditions such as TMJ disorder
•	Called upon to refer patients to different dentists (oral surgeons, orthodontists etc.)
•	Injured in accidents
•	Patients that need TMJ and implant tomography, orthodontic radiographic examinations, diagnostic consultation, nuclear medicine ultrasound

    Historical background and evolution of specialty

    Initial logo of American Academy of Dental Radiology in 1968 
    •	X-ray first discovered in 1895
•	First dental radiograph made the following year in 1896
•	First dental x-ray lab in US opened in 1903 by Kells
•	Throughout the years, technique gradually improved
•	1925, bite-wing technique, 1940 long cone design, 1941 ultra-speed film introduced
•	1949 The American Academy of Dental Roentgenologists was established
•	In the same year, the first organizational meeting was held in San Francisco
•	1951 the name was changed to American Academy of Oral Roentgenology
•	1963 AAOR supported by national institution of dental research, first indication that 
	Oral Roentgenology was recognized by NIDR as a dental discipline of interest
•	1968, name changed to American Academy of Dental Radiology
•	1968, AADR applied for specialty status
•	1969, ADA Council on Dental Education disapproved application for specialty status, numerous appeals and rejection took place
•	1971, Academy decided on new strategy for specialty recognition, tried to have state 
	delegation to introduce it but was of no avail as the measure was never actually carried out
•	1978 dental xeroradiography introduced, 1987 intraoral digital radiography introduced
•	1989, Name changed to American Academy of Oral and Maxillofacial Radiology
•	1994 ADA denied specialty application of OMR
•	1995, application for OMR specialty recognition submitted to ADA
•	1995 first digital sensor for panoramic unit introduced, cone beam CT scanners available in USA in 2001
•	1996 approval of OMR specialty recognition recommended granting recognition to 
	OMR by ADA’s Council on Dental Education and Licensure (CDEL). Approved, following morning, second vote
•	overturned the previous one. Later that day, another motion to reconsider was 
	called upon and referred back to the council with a request to make another 
	recommendation next year (so not approved)
•	1997 OMR application for specialty recognition was denied, appealed but ADA house decision was not reversed
•	1998 application specialty recognition was submitted to ADA
•	1999 Oral and Maxillofacial Radiology became the 9th ADA recognized specialty on October 13th

    Information for education

    •	Requires a DDS/DDM degree
•	2 year certificate program or 3-4 year graduate degree for teaching and researching career
•	Clinical courses might include diagnostic techniques, special procedures and radiation biology
•	Take state or national board exams
•	Continuing education classes OMR often cover latest treatment methods
•	Need to be computer literate and possess an astute visual memory and high level of manual dexterity
•	Digital imaging becoming more prevalent, dental radiologist will likely to be more 
	proficient with programs that allow x-rays to be digitally uploaded to computer and 
	other programs that comes with it
